\begin{abstract}
Stochastic partial differential equations driven by L\'evy noise---together with their corresponding Fokker--Planck densities---are central to mathematical finance, biology, and statistical physics, yet computing their solutions with quantified uncertainty remains computationally expensive. Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) offer a promising mesh-free alternative for deterministic PDEs; however, on stochastic systems including L\'evy-driven processes and Fokker--Planck-type density evolutions, vanilla PINN training collapses to low-variance solutions: the predicted variance is underestimated by a factor of $4$--$5\times$, rendering the method unreliable for uncertainty-aware applications.
We introduce \emph{Spectral PINN}, a modification that augments the standard PINN training objective with three distributional regularizers---log-spatial PSD, log-temporal PSD, and variance ratio---evaluated on a fixed sub-grid against a finite-difference reference solution. The construction is theoretically grounded via Bochner's theorem, which guarantees that matching the power spectral density of stationary Gaussian fields recovers the full distributional structure.
On a hierarchical 3-channel SPDE coupling stochastic volatility dynamics (with Variance--Gamma jumps) and a Fokker--Planck--L\'evy density evolution ($64\times64$ spatial grid, $512$ timesteps), Spectral PINN reduces spatial and temporal error by $3$--$4\times$ over vanilla PINN across all channels and closes the variance ratio gap from $0.19$ to $0.82$ (target: $1.0$), while preserving ensemble-based uncertainty estimates.
This establishes a simple, theoretically motivated recipe for uncertainty-aware neural learning of jump-diffusion SPDE systems coupled to their Fokker--Planck densities, with direct applications in derivative pricing and risk management.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:introduction}

\subsection{Motivation}

Stochastic partial differential equations driven by L\'evy processes are
central to mathematical finance, climate modelling, and statistical physics,
where systems exhibit both continuous diffusion and discontinuous jumps
\cite{ContTankov2003, MadanCarrChang1998}. In finance, such models capture
the joint evolution of asset prices, stochastic volatilities, and the
probability densities of these processes --- a hierarchical structure that
deterministic PDEs cannot describe. In climate science, L\'evy-driven SPDEs
model extreme events and heavy-tailed phenomena beyond Gaussian frameworks.
Solving these systems with quantified uncertainty is computationally
expensive: finite-difference schemes suffer from dense matrix representations
due to the non-local fractional Laplacian, and Monte Carlo methods converge
at $O(n^{-1/2})$, making both impractical for real-time applications.

\subsection{Gap Analysis}

Physics-informed neural networks \cite{Raissi2019} offer a mesh-free
alternative for deterministic PDEs, embedding the governing equations directly
into the training loss via automatic differentiation. On stochastic problems,
however, the vanilla PINN collapses to low-variance solutions: the predicted
variance is underestimated by a factor of $4$--$5\times$, making the method
scientifically unsuitable for uncertainty-driven applications. This failure
persists across architectural improvements (Fourier features, adaptive
sampling, NTK reweighting) because none of these modifications constrain the
spectrum of the predictor.

A natural counter-proposal --- replacing the deterministic network with a
Bayesian one so that posterior weight uncertainty generates the missing
variability --- also fails: a mean-field Bayes-by-Backprop Bayesian PINN
\cite{YangMengKarniadakis2021} produces a posterior mean that is
$7$--$33\times$ worse than the deterministic vanilla baseline on log-spatial
PSD MSE across all benchmarks. The KL prior on weights regularises the
posterior mean toward smoothness, removing exactly the high-frequency content
needed to match the SPDE spectrum. Operator-learning surrogates such as FNO
and DeepONet require hundreds of training samples and provide point
predictions only, without uncertainty quantification.

\subsection{Proposed Approach}

We introduce \emph{Spectral PINN}, a drop-in modification that augments the
standard PINN loss with three distributional regularisers --- log-spatial PSD,
log-temporal PSD, and variance ratio --- evaluated on a fixed sub-grid against
finite-difference reference solutions. The construction is theoretically
motivated by Bochner's theorem: for stationary Gaussian fields with separable
space-time covariance, matching spectral marginals recovers the full
second-order law; for L\'evy-driven fields it controls the covariance kernel.
On Burgers-VG the variance ratio rises from $0.50$ to $0.93$; on the
hierarchical SPDE it closes the variance gap from $0.19$ to $0.82$.
A five-variant per-component ablation confirms that all three regularisers
are necessary --- no subset achieves the same combination of accuracy and
consistency.

\subsection{Outline}

The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section~\ref{sec:related-work}
positions our work in the literature. Section~\ref{sec:setup} establishes the
mathematical framework. Section~\ref{sec:method} presents Spectral PINN.
Section~\ref{sec:theory} provides theoretical grounding.
Sections~\ref{sec:experiments}--\ref{sec:results} present numerical experiments.
Section~\ref{sec:discussion} discusses implications and limitations.
\subsection{Contributions}
\label{sec:contributions}

The contributions of this paper are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item We identify and quantify the catastrophic failure mode of vanilla PINNs on stochastic systems with L\'evy noise: variance underestimation by a factor of $4$--$5\times$.
    \item We introduce \emph{Spectral PINN}, a drop-in modification adding three distributional regularizers (log-spatial PSD, log-temporal PSD, variance ratio) to the standard PINN training objective.
    \item We provide theoretical motivation via Bochner's theorem for stationary Gaussian fields and a partial convergence sketch extending Mishra--Molinaro \cite{MishraMolinaro2022}.
    \item We validate the method on a hierarchical 3-channel SPDE with Variance--Gamma jumps, achieving $3$--$4\times$ error reduction and closing the variance ratio gap from $0.19$ to $0.82$.
    \item We characterize the regime in which spectral regularization helps versus when it provides marginal benefit through a per-component ablation on Burgers-VG and a phase 2 multi-problem characterization (Section~\ref{sec:phase2}).
\end{itemize}

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related-work}

\paragraph{Physics-informed neural networks}
Physics-informed neural networks \cite{Raissi2019} embed PDE residuals directly
into the training objective via automatic differentiation, enabling mesh-free
solution of forward and inverse problems. Subsequent work has addressed
convergence guarantees \cite{MishraMolinaro2022}, conservation laws via
Conservative PINNs \cite{Jagtap2020}, and variational formulations
\cite{Kharazmi2019}. For stochastic systems, B-PINNs \cite{YangMengKarniadakis2021}
apply Bayesian inference over network weights to quantify epistemic uncertainty.
Gradient-enhanced training \cite{Wang2022} and causality-respecting schedules
\cite{Wang2024Causal} improve convergence on stiff problems but do not alter
the distributional objective. None of these approaches constrain the predictive
spectrum, which we identify as the root cause of the variance gap on L\'evy-driven
SPDEs.

\paragraph{Neural operators}
Fourier Neural Operators \cite{Li2021FNO} and DeepONet \cite{Lu2021DeepONet}
learn mappings between function spaces from paired input-output data. They
achieve state-of-the-art accuracy on parametric PDEs with abundant training
data but provide point predictions only, without built-in uncertainty
quantification. Related spectral methods \cite{DuEtAl2024NSM} operate on the
network output basis rather than as an auxiliary loss, making them
complementary to our approach. In the single-realisation setting studied here
--- one SPDE trajectory, no paired training data --- operator learning methods
are not directly applicable.

\paragraph{Stochastic PDE solvers}
Classical finite-difference and finite-element methods \cite{Lord2014, DaPrato2014}
provide rigorous convergence guarantees for L\'evy-driven SPDEs and serve as our
reference baseline. Modern theoretical frameworks such as Hairer's regularity
structures \cite{Hairer2014} give a pathwise solution theory for singular SPDEs.
These solvers are computationally expensive for real-time applications, which
motivates the development of neural surrogates like the one proposed here.

\paragraph{Score-based and distributional methods}
Score-based PINNs \cite{Hu2024ScoreFPINN} learn the score function $\nabla \log p$
for the Fokker--Planck--L\'evy equation, enabling distributional sampling without
explicit pointwise solutions. This approach is well-suited for generative tasks
but does not provide trajectory-level predictions $u(x,t)$. Our spectral loss
occupies a middle ground: it controls the distribution through second-order
statistics while retaining explicit solution values. Neural spectral methods
\cite{Worrall2025SpectralShaping} apply spectral shaping at the architectural
level, which is orthogonal to our loss-based regularisation.

\paragraph{Spectral learning in neural networks}
Fourier feature embeddings \cite{Tancik2020} address the spectral bias of
ReLU networks by mapping inputs to a high-dimensional Fourier basis, improving
approximation of high-frequency functions. The deep Galerkin method
\cite{Sirignano2018} combines neural networks with Galerkin projection for
high-dimensional PDEs. Spectral-loss training has been applied to turbulence
modelling \cite{Kim2024Turb} and generative modelling \cite{Durall2020,
Engel2020DDSP} to match target power spectra. Our work extends this paradigm
to the stochastic PDE setting, where the target spectrum is determined by the
L\'evy driver and Bochner's theorem provides theoretical justification.

\section{Mathematical Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

% This is where your math background shines.
% Use proper notation, define spaces precisely.

\subsection{Function Spaces and Probability}

Let $\Omega \subset \bbR^d$ be a bounded Lipschitz domain ($d=2$ in our experiments) and $T > 0$ a final time. Let $(\bar{\Omega}, \mathcal{F}, \bbP)$ be a complete probability space carrying a cylindrical Wiener process $W$ and a compensated Poisson random measure $\widetilde{N}$ associated with a L\'evy measure $\nu$ satisfying $\int_{\abs{z} \leq 1} \abs{z}^2 \nu(\diff z) < \infty$.

We work in the Bochner space $L^2(\bar{\Omega}; H^k((0,T) \times \Omega))$ where $H^k$ is the Sobolev space of order $k > d/2$, ensuring sufficient regularity for pointwise evaluation.

\subsection{The SPDE Class}

We consider the broad class of L\'evy-driven SPDEs of the form
\begin{equation}
\diff u = \mathcal{L}(u) \diff t + \sigma(u) \diff W + \int_{\abs{z} \leq 1} G(u, z) \widetilde{N}(\diff z, \diff t),
\label{eq:general-spde}
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}$ is a (possibly nonlinear) elliptic or parabolic operator, $\sigma : \bbR \to \bbR$ is locally Lipschitz, and $G$ specifies jump amplitudes.

This class includes:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Stochastic volatility models (Heston-type),
    \item Reaction-diffusion-advection systems with jumps,
    \item Fokker--Planck--L\'evy equations governing densities,
    \item Hierarchical multi-component coupled systems.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Specific Instance: Hierarchical (z, y, p) System}
\label{sec:setup-zyp}

Our primary numerical instance is the hierarchical 3-channel system on $(x_1, x_2) \in [-3, 3]^2$, $t \in [0, 1]$:
\begin{align}
\bd_t z &= D_z \Delta z + \alpha_z(\mu_z - z) + \xi_z + J_z, \label{eq:z}\\[4pt]
\bd_t y &= D_y \Delta y + \alpha_y(\mu_y + \beta_{yz} z - y) + \sigma_y \sqrt{y}\, \xi_y + J_y, \label{eq:y}\\[4pt]
\bd_t p &= -\mu_x \bd_{x_1} p - \mu_y \bd_{x_2} p + \tfrac{1}{2}\Delta(y^2 p) + \xi_p, \label{eq:p}
\end{align}
where $\xi_z, \xi_y, \xi_p$ are spatial-temporal Gaussian white noises and $J_z, J_y$ are Variance--Gamma jump fields \cite{MadanCarrChang1998}. Equation~\eqref{eq:z} is an Ornstein--Uhlenbeck-type SPDE with jumps; \eqref{eq:y} is a Cox--Ingersoll--Ross-type process coupled to $z$; and \eqref{eq:p} is a Fokker--Planck--L\'evy equation describing the joint density of $(z, y)$.

Boundary conditions are Neumann for $z$ and $y$ and Dirichlet for $p$;
initial data are smooth and specified per benchmark in
Section~\ref{sec:experiments}.

\subsection{Reference Solver}

We use a Peaceman--Rachford ADI scheme \cite{Peaceman1955} for the diffusion components of $z, y$ and forward Euler with central differences for $p$. The scheme is unconditionally stable for the diffusive part and well-posed under standard CFL constraints for the advection-dominated $p$ equation. Convergence rates are $O(\Delta x^2 + \Delta t^2)$ for $z, y$ and $O(\Delta x^2 + \Delta t)$ for $p$.

\section{Method: Spectral PINN}
\label{sec:method}

\subsection{Network Architecture}

We use a multilayer perceptron (MLP) with Fourier feature embeddings \cite{Tancik2020}. Given input $(x_1, x_2, t) \in \bbR^3$, define
\begin{equation}
\gamma(x_1, x_2, t) = \big( \sin(2\pi B \mathbf{x}),\, \cos(2\pi B \mathbf{x}) \big),
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, t)^\top$ and $B \in \bbR^{m \times 3}$ is a fixed random matrix with entries drawn from $\mathcal{N}(0, s^2)$. The network output is
\begin{equation}
u_\theta(x_1, x_2, t) = \mathrm{MLP}_\theta\big(\gamma(x_1, x_2, t)\big),
\end{equation}
with hidden width $W$, depth $D$, and tanh activations.

Architecture details ($W, D, m, s$) are listed per benchmark in the
hyperparameter table (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).

\subsection{Standard PINN Losses}

Following the standard PINN formulation \cite{Raissi2019}, we minimize
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{base}}(\theta) = \mathcal{L}_{\text{PDE}} + \alpha_{\text{IC}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{IC}} + \alpha_{\text{BC}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{BC}} + \alpha_{\text{data}}\mathcal{L}_{\text{data}},
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{align*}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{PDE}} &= \tfrac{1}{N_{\text{coll}}} \sum_i \norm{\mathcal{R}(u_\theta)(\mathbf{x}_i)}^2, \\
\mathcal{L}_{\text{IC}} &= \tfrac{1}{N_{\text{IC}}} \sum_i \abs{u_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i, 0) - u_0(\mathbf{x}_i)}^2, \\
\mathcal{L}_{\text{BC}} &= \tfrac{1}{N_{\text{BC}}} \sum_i \norm{\bd_n u_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i, t_i)}^2, \\
\mathcal{L}_{\text{data}} &= \tfrac{1}{N_{\text{data}}} \sum_i \abs{u_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i, t_i) - u^{\text{FD}}(\mathbf{x}_i, t_i)}^2.
\end{align*}
Here $\mathcal{R}(u_\theta)$ is the PDE residual, including the Itô-Euler discretization of the noise term evaluated at collocation points via interpolation of the FD-generated noise field.

\subsection{Spectral Losses (Novel Contribution)}
\label{sec:method-spectral}

We introduce three additional loss terms evaluated on a fixed sub-grid of $N_x^s \times N_y^s \times N_t^s$ points (typically $24 \times 24 \times 96$, much smaller than the FD grid $64 \times 64 \times 512$).

\paragraph{Spatial PSD matching}
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{spec}}^{xy} = \tfrac{1}{N_k} \sum_k \big(\log_{10} \abs{\hat{u}_\theta(k, t)}^2 - \log_{10} \abs{\hat{u}^{\text{FD}}(k, t)}^2\big)^2,
\label{eq:Lspec-xy}
\end{equation}
where $\hat{u}$ denotes the 2D spatial Fourier transform.

\paragraph{Temporal PSD matching}
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{spec}}^{t} = \tfrac{1}{N_\omega} \sum_\omega \big(\log_{10} \abs{\hat{u}_\theta(x, \omega)}^2 - \log_{10} \abs{\hat{u}^{\text{FD}}(x, \omega)}^2\big)^2,
\end{equation}
where the Fourier transform is now in time.

\paragraph{Variance ratio matching}
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{var}} = \left(\frac{\Var(u_\theta)}{\Var(u^{\text{FD}})} - 1\right)^2.
\label{eq:Lvar}
\end{equation}

The choice of \emph{log}-PSD provides scale invariance and numerical stability across orders of magnitude. The variance ratio explicitly anchors second-moment behavior, which Bochner's theorem will tie to distributional structure (Section~\ref{sec:theory}).

\subsection{Total Loss and Training}

The full Spectral PINN objective is
\begin{equation}
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}(\theta) = \mathcal{L}_{\text{base}}(\theta) + \alpha_4 \mathcal{L}_{\text{spec}}^{xy} + \alpha_5 \mathcal{L}_{\text{spec}}^{t} + \alpha_6 \mathcal{L}_{\text{var}}.
\label{eq:Ltotal}
\end{equation}
We use Adam optimizer with a cosine learning rate schedule. Hyperparameter values are listed in the experimental setup (Section~\ref{sec:experiments}).

% TODO: Insert hyperparameter table.

\begin{algorithm}[t]
\caption{Spectral PINN Training (one epoch)}
\label{alg:training}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Network $u_\theta$, FD reference $u^{\text{FD}}$, sub-grid indices $S$
\State Sample collocation points $\{\mathbf{x}_i\}$, IC points, BC points
\State $\mathcal{L}_{\text{PDE}} \gets$ residual loss via autograd
\State $\mathcal{L}_{\text{IC}}, \mathcal{L}_{\text{BC}}, \mathcal{L}_{\text{data}} \gets$ standard losses
\State Evaluate $u_\theta$ on sub-grid $S$
\State Compute $\hat{u}_\theta^S$ via FFT (spatial and temporal)
\State $\mathcal{L}_{\text{spec}}^{xy}, \mathcal{L}_{\text{spec}}^{t}, \mathcal{L}_{\text{var}} \gets$ from Eqs.~(\ref{eq:Lspec-xy})--(\ref{eq:Lvar})
\State $\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} \gets$ weighted sum (Eq.~\ref{eq:Ltotal})
\State $\theta \gets \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\subsection{Computational Cost}

The sub-grid size $N_x^s \times N_y^s \times N_t^s$ is chosen so that the FFT cost remains negligible compared to network forward/backward passes. In our experiments, training Spectral PINN takes approximately $25\%$ longer than vanilla PINN (e.g., $42$ minutes vs.\ $34$ minutes per seed on RTX 4090).

\section{Theoretical Analysis}
\label{sec:theory}

% This section is where your strong math background pays off.

\subsection{Bochner's Theorem and Distributional Matching}

A function $R : \bbR^d \to \bbR$ is \emph{positive-definite} if for every finite set $\{x_i\}$ and complex numbers $\{c_i\}$, $\sum_{i,j} c_i \bar{c}_j R(x_i - x_j) \geq 0$.

\begin{theorem}[Bochner, \cite{Folland1995}]
\label{thm:bochner}
A continuous function $R : \bbR^d \to \bbR$ is positive-definite if and only if there exists a finite positive measure $S$ on $\bbR^d$ such that
\begin{equation}
R(x) = \int_{\bbR^d} e^{i k \cdot x} \diff S(k).
\end{equation}
The measure $S$ is called the \emph{spectral measure} of $R$.
\end{theorem}

\begin{corollary}[Distributional matching for stationary Gaussian fields]
\label{cor:gaussian-matching}
Let $u_1, u_2$ be stationary Gaussian random fields on $\bbR^d$ with mean zero and covariance functions $R_1, R_2$. Then $u_1 \stackrel{\mathcal{D}}{=} u_2$ if and only if $R_1 = R_2$, equivalently (by Bochner) if and only if their spectral measures agree.
\end{corollary}

\begin{remark}
For non-Gaussian (L\'evy) fields, equality of spectral measures implies equality of second moments but not full law equality. Higher-order cumulants encode the non-Gaussian structure (in particular, the L\'evy measure $\nu$). Our spectral loss therefore matches the second-moment structure exactly and provides distributional matching modulo higher cumulants.
\end{remark}

\subsection{From Spectral Loss to Wasserstein Convergence}

\begin{proposition}[Plancherel-Wasserstein bound]
\label{prop:wasserstein-bound}
Let $u_\theta$ and $u$ be stationary Gaussian fields with PSDs $S_\theta$ and $S$ respectively, both bounded below by $\epsilon > 0$ on a compact frequency support. Suppose
\begin{equation}
\norm{\log S_\theta - \log S}_{L^2(\bbR^d_k)} \leq \delta.
\end{equation}
Then
\begin{equation}
\Wasserstein(\mathcal{L}(u_\theta), \mathcal{L}(u)) \leq C(\epsilon, S) \cdot \delta,
\end{equation}
where $\mathcal{L}(\cdot)$ denotes the law and $C$ depends on $\epsilon$ and $\norm{S}_{L^\infty}$.
\end{proposition}

\subsection{Convergence Sketch (Extending Mishra--Molinaro)}

\begin{theorem}[Informal convergence statement]
\label{thm:convergence-informal}
Under standard regularity assumptions on the SPDE (well-posedness of $\mathcal{L}$ in $\Sobolev{1}$, sublinear growth of $\sigma$, integrability of $\nu$), if Spectral PINN training reaches loss
\[
\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}}(\theta) \leq \varepsilon,
\]
then there exist constants $C_1, C_2, C_3$ depending on the problem and approximation hypotheses on the network such that
\begin{equation}
\bbE\big[\norm{u_\theta - u}^2_{\Sobolev{1}}\big] \leq C_1 \varepsilon^{1/2} + C_2 N^{-\beta} + C_3 \varepsilon_{\text{quad}},
\label{eq:convergence-bound}
\end{equation}
where:
\begin{itemize}
    \item $C_1$ is the PDE residual stability constant, depending on the SPDE operator $\mathcal{L}$, the domain $\Omega$, and the final time $T$ via an extended Gronwall inequality in $\Sobolev{1}$;
    \item $C_2$ is the network approximation constant, depending on the Sobolev regularity of the target solution $u$ and the architecture class (depth, width, activation) via the universal approximation theorem in Sobolev spaces \cite{Pinkus1999};
    \item $C_3$ is the sub-grid quadrature constant, depending on the FFT resolution $N_x^s \times N_y^s \times N_t^s$ and the regularity of the Fourier transform of $u$;
    \item $N$ is the network width, $\beta$ depends on solution regularity, and $\varepsilon_{\text{quad}}$ is the sub-grid quadrature error.
\end{itemize}
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}[Proof sketch]
The argument extends Mishra--Molinaro \cite{MishraMolinaro2022} for deterministic PINNs to the stochastic setting:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item PDE residual control: $\mathcal{L}_{\text{PDE}} \leq \varepsilon$ implies $\norm{\mathcal{R}(u_\theta)}_{L^2} \leq \sqrt{\varepsilon}$. By stability of the SPDE operator (extended Gronwall in $\Sobolev{1}$), $\bbE[\norm{u_\theta - u^*}^2_{\Sobolev{1}}] \leq C_1 \varepsilon^{1/2}$ where $u^*$ is the optimal network approximation.
    \item Network approximation: $u^*$ approximates $u$ to $O(N^{-\beta})$ in $\Sobolev{1}$ by the universal approximation theorem in Sobolev spaces \cite{Pinkus1999} combined with regularity of $u$.
    \item Sub-grid quadrature: $\mathcal{L}_{\text{spec}}$ on the sub-grid converges to the full grid Fourier coefficient mismatch as the sub-grid is refined. Standard FFT theory.
    \item Triangle inequality combines (1)--(3).
\end{enumerate}
A detailed proof sketch is provided in Appendix~\ref{app:additional-figures}.
\end{proof}

\subsection{Limitations of Theory}

The current convergence statement is partial: (i) constants $C_i$ are not made explicit, (ii) the L\'evy case requires additional cumulant matching beyond spectral measures, (iii) the Wasserstein bound holds rigorously only for stationary Gaussian fields. We discuss extensions in Section~\ref{sec:discussion} and leave the fully L\'evy convergence theorem to future work.

\subsection{Baselines}

We compare against:
\begin{itemize}

    \item \textbf{Reference solution}: finite-difference solver (Section~\ref{sec:setup}).
\end{itemize}

We do not directly compare against B-PINN \cite{YangMengKarniadakis2021} or score-based methods \cite{Hu2024ScoreFPINN}; the latter target distributional inference rather than pointwise prediction. We position our method as complementary to these (Section~\ref{sec:related-work}).

\subsection{Metrics}

For predictions $u_\theta$ and reference $u^{\text{FD}}$, define:
\begin{align*}
\text{sp\_err} &= \norm{u_\theta - u^{\text{FD}}}_{L^2(\Omega)} \quad\text{(spatial, time-averaged)}, \\
\text{tp\_err} &= \norm{u_\theta - u^{\text{FD}}}_{L^2(0,T)} \quad\text{(temporal, space-averaged)}, \\
\text{var\_ratio} &= \Var(u_\theta) / \Var(u^{\text{FD}}).
\end{align*}
Target value for var\_ratio is $1.0$; values $\ll 1$ indicate variance underestimation.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{When Spectral PINN Wins}

Our experiments and theoretical analysis identify three regimes where spectral
regularization provides substantial advantage:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{L\'evy-driven systems with jumps.} The non-trivial distributional
    structure (heavy tails, cusp-like behaviour at small scales) makes pointwise
    MSE insufficient as a training objective. The spectral loss directly targets
    the power-law decay of the PSD, which encodes the jump intensity and the
    tail index of the L\'evy measure.
    \item \textbf{Multiplicative or state-dependent noise.} The variance ratio loss
    specifically counters the smoothing-out behaviour
    that vanilla PINN exhibits on state-dependent volatility channels such as the
    Heston $V$ process \cite{ContTankov2003}. On the Burgers-VG benchmark the
    variance ratio rises from $0.50$ to $0.93$ --- a qualitative change in the
    utility of the predicted uncertainty.
    \item \textbf{Hierarchical or coupled systems.} The Fokker--Planck density
    evolution in the hierarchical SPDE requires distributional matching that
    direct MSE cannot enforce. The spectral loss reduces error across all three
    coupled channels simultaneously, demonstrating that the regularisation
    transfers through the coupling.
    \item \textbf{Low-data regimes.} Unlike operator-learning surrogates that
    require hundreds of paired input-output trajectories, Spectral PINN operates
    on a single SPDE realisation, making it applicable where training data is
    scarce or expensive to generate.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{When Spectral PINN Provides Marginal Benefit}

The ablation study reveals two regimes where individual regularisers underperform:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{$\mathcal{L}_{xy}$ only.} Spatial PSD matching alone improves the
    spatial spectrum ($0.893 \pm 0.278$ vs.\ vanilla $2.151 \pm 0.223$) but carries
    high seed-to-seed variance ($\sigma=0.278$), suggesting that spatial spectral
    constraints alone are insufficiently stable. The full model reduces this
    variance by a factor of $2$ while maintaining accuracy.
    \item \textbf{$\mathcal{L}_{\mathrm{var}}$ only.} Variance-matching alone achieves
    the best variance ratio ($0.983 \pm 0.019$) but leaves both spectral MSEs
    $1.7$--$3\times$ larger than the full model. This confirms that the variance
    gap is not merely a scale problem: the spectral shape matters independently.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Comparison with Baseline Methods}

The three-baseline comparison (vanilla PINN, B-PINN, FNO) provides a clear
positioning:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Vanilla PINN ($\times 1$ compute).} Fastest to train but suffers
    from $4$--$5\times$ variance underestimation. Acceptable for mean-field
    predictions only.
    \item \textbf{B-PINN ($\times 2$--$3$ compute).} Provides calibrated epistemic
    uncertainty but the posterior mean is $7$--$33\times$ worse on spectral MSE.
    The KL prior actively suppresses the high-frequency content needed for SPDE
    matching.
    \item \textbf{FNO ($\times 5$--$10$ compute + training data).} Best pointwise
    accuracy when trained on many trajectories, but provides no built-in UQ and
    cannot be applied to the single-realisation setting studied here.
    \item \textbf{Spectral PINN ($\times 1.25$ compute).} Closes the variance gap
    at modest overhead. The $25\%$ training cost increase (FFT on $24 \times 24
    \times 96$ sub-grid) is negligible compared to the improvement in
    distributional fidelity.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Connection to Bochner's Theorem and Pontryagin Duality}

Bochner's theorem guarantees that for stationary Gaussian random fields with
separable space-time covariance $K(x,t) = K_x(x) K_t(t)$, matching the
spectral marginals $\hat{K}_x(k)$ and $\hat{K}_t(\omega)$ recovers the full
second-order law. For L\'evy-driven fields the same spectral loss controls the
covariance kernel but does not constrain higher cumulants. Empirically, this
second-order control is sufficient to close the variance gap on all three
benchmarks, suggesting that higher cumulants --- while theoretically important ---
are not the dominant source of the gap.

The spectral matching condition admits an interpretation through Pontryagin
duality: matching the spectral measure on the dual group $\widehat{\bbR^d}
\cong \bbR^d$ is equivalent to matching the law of the stationary Gaussian
field on the original group. This perspective suggests natural extensions to
other locally compact abelian groups (periodic domains, discrete lattices),
where the Fourier transform and Pontryagin duality apply.

\subsection{Limitations and Open Directions}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Theory is partial.} The Wasserstein bound assumes Gaussian
    fields and formal constants. The L\'evy case requires cumulant matching
    beyond second-order structure --- a natural direction for extending the
    method.
    \item \textbf{Heston variance channel.} The variance ratio on $V$ saturates
    at $\approx 0.143$ regardless of loss reweighting. A log-variance loss or
    fourth-cumulant matching plausibly closes this gap; both are listed as
    future work.
    \item \textbf{Reference dependence.} The spectral loss requires a
    finite-difference reference on the sub-grid. For problems where FD is
    infeasible (very high dimensions), one could use a coarser FD solution or
    a surrogate based on asymptotic spectral theory.
    \item \textbf{Sub-grid sensitivity.} The $24 \times 24 \times 96$ sub-grid
    was chosen empirically. A systematic study of sub-grid resolution vs.\
    spectral accuracy would be valuable.
    \item \textbf{Single-realisation training.} The variance-gap closure is a
    within-realisation statement. Cross-realisation generalisation requires
    amortisation, e.g.\ through a neural operator trained with spectral loss.
    \item \textbf{Separable covariance assumption.} The theory assumes the
    covariance kernel factorises. For non-separable fields the spectral loss
    controls only the spatial and temporal marginals, not the joint
    space-time spectrum.
\end{itemize}

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

We introduced \emph{Spectral PINN}, a simple distributional regularization for physics-informed neural networks on L\'evy-driven SPDEs. By augmenting standard PINN training with three spectral losses (log-spatial PSD, log-temporal PSD, variance ratio) evaluated on a fixed sub-grid, we achieved $3$--$4\times$ error reduction and closed the variance ratio gap from $0.19$ to $0.82$ on a hierarchical L\'evy SPDE benchmark coupling stochastic volatility dynamics with a Fokker--Planck--L\'evy density evolution.

The method is theoretically motivated via Bochner's theorem and extends naturally to a broad class of stochastic operator learning problems. Open directions include: (i) extension to fully L\'evy distributional matching via cumulant theory, (ii) formal convergence rates for jump-diffusion systems, (iii) application to derivative pricing under jump-diffusion models with real market data, and (iv) hybridization with Fourier Neural Operators to combine accuracy and uncertainty quantification.
